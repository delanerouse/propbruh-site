Human behavior
New York desks go dark
The traders running the biggest desks at major banks, hedge funds, and market makers physically leave between noon and 1pm. These are the institutional participants whose order flow drives directional momentum on ES. When they step away, the orders stop flowing. It's that simple.
Market structure
London closes its session
London is the second-largest financial center in the world and a major source of ES volume during the morning session. By noon New York time it's 5pm in London. Their session is over. That entire pool of institutional participation disappears at the exact same time New York goes to lunch.
Liquidity
Market makers pull back
Firms that provide liquidity — sitting on both sides of the market constantly — reduce activity during lunch because getting caught in a large move with thin order books is dangerous. They widen spreads and reduce size. Less liquidity begets less liquidity.
Catalysts
No news, no fuel
Major economic releases hit at 8:30am, 9:00am, or 10:00am. By noon the morning data is priced in and the next meaningful catalyst — if there is one — is later in the afternoon. With nothing to react to and nobody to react to it, price just drifts.

Self-reinforcing cycle
Everyone knows, so nobody shows up
Experienced traders know lunch is thin, so they don't trade it. Because they don't trade it, it stays thin. Everyone knows everyone else knows — so nobody shows up, and the cycle repeats every single trading day. Your ORB edge requires momentum and institutional participation. Lunch has neither.
